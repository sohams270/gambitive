**The number first, because that's the rule in this section: ₹4.2 crore spent, 14 paying customers promised in the pipeline, 0 purchase orders signed. Eighteen months.**

I want to be precise about what went wrong, because "we built something nobody wanted" is the lazy version, and the lazy version teaches nothing.

People wanted it. That was the trap. I did forty-one discovery calls before writing code. Twelve of them ended with some version of "if you build this, we're in." Procurement heads at real companies, real budgets, visibly frustrated with their existing process. I have the recordings. The enthusiasm was not imagined.

The warning sign was in month two, and I documented it myself without seeing it. My notes from a pilot conversation: "Loves the product. Says procurement tooling decisions go through IT committee, 9–14 month cycle. Suggests we talk to committee in Q3." I logged that as a positive signal. It appears, nearly verbatim, in nine other sets of call notes.

The number I ignored: my average buyer needed eleven months and four internal approvals to spend ₹8 lakh a year with me. My runway was fourteen months. Those two numbers were never compatible, and I knew both of them by month two.

What I did instead of confronting that: I built more product. Every quarter the committee cycle slipped, I added features, on the theory that a more complete product would shortcut the approval process. It does not. Committees do not move faster for better software; the committee *is* the product you have to build for, and I never did.

The money went where you'd expect. Roughly ₹1.8 crore in engineering salaries, ₹80 lakh on two enterprise sales hires I brought in a year too early, ₹40 lakh on a rebrand I approved during the worst quarter — which I can only explain as buying a feeling of progress — and the rest on the ordinary overhead of a company preparing for customers that were permanently two quarters away.

The end was undramatic. We had four months of runway, my lead investor asked me to walk him through the pipeline name by name, and doing that exercise honestly out loud was the first time I heard it the way an outsider would. Fourteen companies, zero purchase orders, average age of "active conversation": ten months. He was kind about it. That was worse.

What I'd do differently is specific, not philosophical. I would charge for pilots — any amount; the point is forcing the buyer's payment process to reveal itself early. I would treat "the committee decides in Q3" as a no until proven otherwise. And I would put my sales-cycle assumption and my runway on the same slide, in the same font size, and update both monthly. The two numbers that killed the company never appeared in the same document until the last one.

We returned about ₹60 lakh to investors. Three of the team's engineers are at companies I introduced them to, which is the only part of the wind-down I'm proud of. I'm building again — smaller cheque, shorter sales cycle, and a pilot price on the first call.
