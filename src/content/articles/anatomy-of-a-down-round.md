By the time a down round is announced, the interesting part is over. The announcement is the corpse; the story is the six months of decisions that produced it. We spent the last month reconstructing one such sequence at a mid-stage consumer company — call it Meridian — through conversations with people on all three sides of the table: founders, existing investors, and the incoming lead.

This is not a story about a bad company. Meridian's revenue grew 60% during the period in question. That's precisely what makes it useful: down rounds are usually narrated as punishment for failure, and that framing hides how mechanical the process actually is once a few dominoes fall.

The first domino fell in a board meeting, as it usually does. The company had nine months of runway and a plan to raise at a flat-to-up valuation. One board member — the fund with the most dry powder and the most at stake — pushed for starting the raise immediately rather than waiting two quarters for better numbers. The logic sounded prudent. It was also, functionally, the beginning of the down round.

Here's the mechanism: starting a raise with nine months of runway and soft metrics signals exactly that to every fund you pitch. The market reads runway like a poker tell. Meridian's deck said "growth story"; its timing said "we're nervous." Every serious fund priced the nervousness, not the growth.

Within six weeks, the founders had twenty-one first meetings and two second meetings. This ratio — not valuation offers, which hadn't even arrived yet — was the moment insiders started behaving differently. Follow-on reserves that had been verbally committed became "subject to the round coming together." A key executive asked directly about runway in an all-hands and got a lawyerly answer, and resignations started within the month. Attrition during a raise is not a side effect; it is part of the pricing.

The term sheet, when it came, was 40% below the last round with a 1.5x liquidation preference. The incoming fund's partner was neither predatory nor apologetic about it. His math, shared candidly: comparable public multiples had compressed 55%, the company was burning 1.4x its net new ARR, and he was pricing in six more months of executive attrition that the founders insisted wouldn't happen. It did.

What happened next is the part that never makes the announcement. Three weeks of negotiation moved the headline valuation up 12% — and quietly made the structure worse. The preference stack tightened, the option pool refresh came out of existing shareholders, and an investor-friendly ratchet was added for the next round. The founders "won" the number that would appear in the press and lost the terms that would govern their next three years.

This trade — optics for structure — is the single most consistent pattern in every down round we examined. Founders negotiate the number their employees and the media will see. Funds negotiate the waterfall. Both sides usually get what they optimized for, and only one of them is optimizing for money.

The existing investors' behaviour deserves more scrutiny than it usually gets. Two of Meridian's three major funds took their pro-rata in the new round, which the announcement dutifully described as "strong insider support." The third fund — the one that had pushed to raise early — did not participate, having concluded privately that its position was underwater beyond rescue. Its partner remained on the board and voted to approve the round. Nothing about this is unusual. All of it shapes outcomes.

For employees, the sequence ran in reverse order of information access. Executives repriced their expectations at the first board meeting. Senior managers repriced when reserves got soft. Everyone else found out from the press release, followed by a hastily scheduled all-hands about "the strong vote of confidence this round represents." The company lost 18% of its staff in the following quarter, concentrated among exactly the people with outside options.

And yet — this is the part the schadenfreude industry misses — the round worked. Meridian got thirty months of runway, cut burn multiple to 0.8x, and grew into its new valuation within eighteen months. The down round was not the disaster. The disaster would have been the bridge-to-nowhere convertible the founders almost took instead, which would have deferred the repricing by a year and added a repayment cliff to it.

The uncomfortable summary: a down round is not an event, it's a disclosure. The value was lost months earlier, in market compression and burn decisions. The round merely published it. Founders who understand this negotiate structure instead of optics, brief their executives before their board forces the issue, and treat the announcement as the beginning of the recovery narrative rather than the end of the fundraising one.

What would we tell a founder walking into this sequence today? Start the raise before you need it or after your metrics turn, never in between. Treat verbal reserve commitments as zero until wired. Trade headline valuation for clean structure every single time. And assume your best people are reading your runway as accurately as your investors are — because they are.
