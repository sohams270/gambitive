Every few months the open network publishes a transaction milestone, the ecosystem celebrates, and nobody asks the only question that decides whether any of it persists: who is earning a margin?

We spent three weeks asking exactly that, across seller apps, buyer apps, logistics providers, and the network's own published financials. The short version: money is being made on the network — just not where the narrative points, and not by most of the companies raising on the ONDC story.

The buyer-side apps that dominate the milestone charts are, almost without exception, buying those transactions. Incentive-adjusted take rates on the buyer side are negative for eight of the ten largest participants. This is not a secret inside the ecosystem; it is simply absent from the press releases.

The seller side is more interesting. Mid-sized seller apps serving specific merchant verticals — pharmacies, mobility, food in tier-2 — are quietly profitable on a contribution basis, because they charge merchants for onboarding, catalogue management, and reconciliation rather than betting on transaction take rates.

That's the pattern worth internalizing: on an open network designed to compress transaction margins, the durable revenue is in the unglamorous services around the transaction, not in the transaction itself.

Logistics tells the same story from a different angle. The network's promise was commoditized fulfilment; the reality is that reliable fulfilment providers gained pricing power, because when the marketplace layer thins out, delivery performance becomes the differentiator buyers actually feel.

Then there is reconciliation — the least discussed and most lucrative layer. Multi-party settlements across network participants generate dispute and reconciliation work that did not exist in the closed-marketplace world. Two companies we spoke to built their entire business on this seam. Neither has ever issued a press release.

The incentive cliff is the number to watch. Network-level buyer incentives have stepped down twice in the past year, and each step-down took roughly 30% of transaction volume with it. The volume that survives incentive removal is the network's real size. On current trajectory, that real size is growing — slowly, unevenly, and mostly in categories nobody demos on stage.

Our conclusion after three weeks: the open network is neither the revolution its advocates promise nor the Potemkin village its critics describe. It is infrastructure, and infrastructure rewards the companies that charge for boring things adjacent to it. If you are evaluating an "ONDC play," ignore the network GMV entirely and ask one question: what do they bill for that survives the incentives going to zero?

That question, inconveniently, filters out most of the pitch decks. It also identifies the handful of companies on the network that will still be here in five years.
