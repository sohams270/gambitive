**The numbers: ₹2.3 crore settlement, 11 months from first board conversation to signed separation, roughly 30% of my exec team's output lost to the standoff in between.**

Nobody fires a co-founder over one thing, so I won't pretend there was a single incident. But there was a single moment when it became unavoidable: our Series B lead told me, in a hallway after a board meeting, that the fund had modelled two versions of our next round — one with both founders, one without him — and the second model priced 20% higher.

Hearing your co-founder quantified as a discount does something clarifying and awful.

I want to be fair to him, because these stories are usually told by the winner and it shows. He built our first product with his own hands. The company exists because of him. What he couldn't do was let anyone else build the third version, and by year six the engineering org was structured entirely around routing work away from his vetoes without telling him. I let that structure grow for over a year because dismantling it meant this conversation.

The weekend the board gave me wasn't really about deciding. It was about accepting a decision I'd made a year earlier and had been paying interest on monthly. Three senior engineers had left citing him in their exits. I re-read those exit notes that weekend. That was the whole deliberation.

The mechanics are what nobody tells you. His unvested equity was the easy part. The fight was over the accelerated vesting clause we'd copy-pasted from a template in 2020 without reading, which entitled him to full acceleration on "termination without cause." Defining cause, six years into a friendship, with lawyers on both sides, took five months and most of what was left of the relationship.

We settled: partial acceleration, a ₹2.3 crore cash component paid over two years, mutual non-disparagement. My lawyers called it a good outcome. It was — against the alternative of litigation. Against the alternative of having had a real vesting conversation in year one, it was a ₹2.3 crore template fee.

What it cost beyond money: two quarters where my leadership team's real job was managing around the dispute, a product launch that slipped past a market window we haven't gotten back, and a friendship I still don't have a category for. He and I have spoken twice since signing. Both times were easier than any conversation in our last two years of working together, which tells you what the working relationship had become.

If you take one operational thing from this: read your acceleration clauses now, while you still like each other. Every founder dispute I've since heard of runs through whatever those paragraphs say. Mine were written by a template, and the template didn't know us.

And the decision itself — if your board is presenting you with a weekend deadline, you're not being asked to decide. You're being told the deciding is overdue. The year I spent not choosing was the most expensive line item in this whole story, and it never appeared on any invoice.
